2 · Findings

F1 — On Sonnet, all three skills raise in-set pass-rate; the most specific helps most.
In-set lift: specific +0.080 > general +0.054 > lean +0.019. Held-out is near ceiling (bare 0.935), so lifts there are ≤+0.007.
F2 — Per example, the cheap endpoints' grade does NOT predict whether a skill helps Sonnet.
Correlation of cheap per-task lift with Sonnet's: Haiku r=-0.079, mini r=-0.11. Accuracy of predicting "helps Sonnet" from "helps cheap": Haiku 45%, mini 50% — at/below the 57% / 56% base rate of always guessing "helps".
F3 — Aggregate ranking: Haiku matches Sonnet; mini inverts it.
Haiku in-set: specific +0.061 (best), all positive. mini in-set: specific -0.040 (worst) while Sonnet scores it +0.080 (best).
F4 — The dominant per-example predictor is task difficulty, not the cheap endpoint.
On hard tasks (Sonnet-bare <0.80) a skill helps Sonnet 91% of the time (n=33); on easy tasks (≥0.92), 50%. The headroom is the signal.
F5 — Trajectory-level: mini's behavioral change carries signal its grade does not.
mini's grade-lift correlates -0.11 with Sonnet, but how much longer mini's deliverable gets under the skill correlates 0.236 (in-set) with Sonnet's lift; artifact-count change 0.155. Haiku shows no such effect.

6 · Method & caveats

  • Lift = mean ensemble pass-rate(skill) − mean ensemble pass-rate(bare), per endpoint. "helps" threshold = ±0.02.
  • Per-task effects are small (±0.02–0.08), near the ensemble grading-noise floor (~0.025/task) — so aggregate signal is more reliable than per-example.
  • Sonnet-bare is from the no-skill baseline-100 grading (same ensemble judge); skill arms are freshly graded.
  • Held-out (15 tasks) is near ceiling (bare 0.935), so it mostly tests for regressions, not lift.
  • Coverage: 486/495 deliverables graded; 9 cells lacked a deliverable (incl. 2 haiku+general that produced no output).
  • Grading run synchronously (the ensemble's mixed-model OpenAI batch is invalid; same models/verdicts).
