Can a cheaper signal predict whether a skill helps Sonnet?

45 Harvey legal tasks × 4 skill arms (bare / lean / general / specific). Ground truth = Claude Sonnet 4.6 run as a full agent. Two candidate proxies: (a) revise Sonnet's own no-skill deliverable with the skill in one call; (b) re-run a cheap agent (Claude Haiku 4.5 / OpenAI gpt-5-mini) from scratch. Every deliverable graded by the ensemble judge (gpt-5-mini + gpt-5, escalating to Sonnet on disagreement). Lift = skill pass-rate − no-skill (bare) pass-rate.

Best method found — the revision proxy (§1): having Sonnet revise its own no-skill deliverable with a skill predicts the agent's actual full-run lift at r=0.76 (in-set), at ~4× lower cost than re-running the agent. Re-running a cheap model from scratch (§2–6) does not transfer per example: r≈0. Revise results are folded into every section below (highlighted columns).

1 · Revision proxy — the best predictor (and ~4× cheaper)

Instead of re-running the agent with a skill, take Sonnet's no-skill (bare) deliverable and have a model REVISE it with the skill in a single call (source documents pre-loaded, no tools). The revision's lift tracks the agent's actual full-run lift — the strongest, cheapest signal found here. (Single-call beat an agentic reviser in a 5-task A/B: r=0.86 vs 0.55; 45 tasks × 3 skills × 2 revisers = 270 revisions.)

3 · Findings

F1 — On Sonnet, all three skills raise in-set pass-rate; the most specific helps most.
In-set lift: specific +0.080 > general +0.054 > lean +0.019. Held-out is near ceiling (bare 0.935), so lifts there are ≤+0.007.
F2 — The cheap full-agent grade does NOT predict whether a skill helps Sonnet per example; Sonnet-revise does.
Correlation of per-task lift with Sonnet's: Haiku-agent r=-0.079, mini-agent r=-0.11 — vs Sonnet-revise r=0.76 (in-set). Predicting "helps Sonnet" from "helps cheap agent": Haiku 45%, mini 50% — at/below the 57% / 56% base rate of always guessing "helps".
F3 — Aggregate ranking: Haiku-agent matches Sonnet; mini inverts it; Sonnet-revise matches it.
Haiku-agent in-set: specific +0.061 (best), all positive. mini-agent in-set: specific -0.040 (worst) while Sonnet scores it +0.080 (best). Sonnet-revise also ranks specific highest (§2).
F4 — The dominant per-example predictor is task difficulty, not the cheap endpoint.
On hard tasks (Sonnet-bare <0.80) a skill helps Sonnet 91% of the time (n=33); on easy tasks (≥0.92), 50%. The headroom is the signal.
F5 — Trajectory-level: mini-agent's behavioral change carries signal its grade does not.
mini's grade-lift correlates -0.11 with Sonnet, but how much longer mini's deliverable gets under the skill correlates 0.236 (in-set) with Sonnet's lift; artifact-count change 0.155. Haiku shows no such effect.

7 · Method & caveats

  • Lift = mean ensemble pass-rate(skill) − mean ensemble pass-rate(bare), per endpoint. "helps" threshold = ±0.02.
  • Revision proxy = one Claude call per (task, skill): system = "revise your earlier deliverable to follow this playbook"; user = task + instructions + source documents + the bare deliverable + the skill. No tools; documents pre-loaded; 1h prompt-cache on the shared prefix. Revise lift = graded revised pass-rate − graded bare pass-rate, same ensemble judge.
  • Per-task effects are small (±0.02–0.08), near the ensemble grading-noise floor (~0.025/task) — so aggregate signal is more reliable than per-example.
  • Sonnet-bare is from the no-skill baseline-100 grading (same ensemble judge); skill arms are freshly graded.
  • Held-out (15 tasks) is near ceiling (bare 0.935), so it mostly tests for regressions, not lift; revise correlations there are ~0 because there is no lift to predict.
  • Coverage: 486/495 full-agent deliverables graded; 9 cells lacked a deliverable. 270/270 revisions graded.
  • Grading run via the ensemble judge; the mixed-model OpenAI batch was fixed to group by model.
